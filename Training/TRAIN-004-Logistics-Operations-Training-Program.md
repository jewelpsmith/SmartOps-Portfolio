## MODULE 1: Logistics and Supply Chain Foundations

### 1.1 The Supply Chain Defined

Most people think of logistics as the movement 
of boxes from one place to another. That is 
accurate but incomplete. Logistics is the 
management of the flow of goods, information, 
and resources from origin to destination in 
a way that is efficient, reliable, and secure.

Understanding the full supply chain helps 
every logistics professional see why their 
individual actions matter beyond their 
immediate task.
```
THE END-TO-END SUPPLY CHAIN

SUPPLIERS
Raw materials; components; goods produced 
by third-party vendors
↓
PROCUREMENT
Purchasing; vendor management; 
contract administration
↓
INBOUND LOGISTICS
Transportation from supplier to warehouse 
or distribution center
↓
WAREHOUSING AND INVENTORY
Storage; organization; 
inventory management and accuracy
↓
OUTBOUND LOGISTICS
Transportation from warehouse or 
distribution center to customer
↓
LAST MILE DELIVERY
Final delivery to end destination; 
most expensive and complex segment per unit
↓
REVERSE LOGISTICS
Returns; exchanges; recycling; 
disposal of goods moving back through the chain

INFORMATION FLOWS IN BOTH DIRECTIONS THROUGHOUT
Purchase orders; shipping confirmations; 
tracking data; invoices; proof of delivery
```

### 1.2 Key Logistics Metrics Every Staff Member Should Know

You cannot improve what you do not measure 
and you cannot measure what you do not understand. 
These are the metrics that define logistics 
performance in this organization.
```
CORE LOGISTICS KPIs

ON-TIME DELIVERY RATE (OTDR)
What it measures: Shipments delivered on or 
                  before committed date
Target:           ≥ 98%
Why it matters:   Most direct measure of 
                  service reliability to customers
Your impact:      Accurate booking; correct carrier 
                  selection; proactive exception management

PERFECT ORDER RATE
What it measures: Orders delivered complete; 
                  on time; undamaged; 
                  with accurate documentation
Target:           ≥ 95%
Why it matters:   Single most comprehensive measure 
                  of end-to-end logistics quality
Your impact:      Documentation accuracy; 
                  shipment verification; 
                  carrier selection quality

FREIGHT COST PER UNIT
What it measures: Total freight cost divided 
                  by units shipped
Target:           [Organization specific]
Why it matters:   Operational efficiency benchmark; 
                  rises when exceptions increase
Your impact:      Carrier selection; 
                  consolidation opportunities; 
                  exception reduction

EXCEPTION RATE
What it measures: Percentage of shipments 
                  experiencing delays; damage; 
                  or delivery failures
Target:           Below 2%
Why it matters:   Exceptions cost 3 to 5 times 
                  more to manage than normal shipments
Your impact:      Carrier performance monitoring; 
                  proactive communication; 
                  root cause identification

DOCK-TO-STOCK TIME
What it measures: Time from inbound receipt 
                  to inventory availability
Target:           [Organization specific; 
                  typically 24 to 48 hours]
Why it matters:   Directly affects fulfillment 
                  capability and customer service
Your impact:      Receiving accuracy; 
                  documentation speed; 
                  system update timeliness

CARRIER SCORECARD AVERAGE
What it measures: Average performance score 
                  across all active carriers 
                  per LOG-001 methodology
Target:           ≥ 80 across all Tier 1 carriers
Why it matters:   Aggregate indicator of 
                  carrier relationship health
Your impact:      Scorecard accuracy; 
                  escalation of underperformance; 
                  relationship management
```

### 1.3 Shipment Types and Terminology
```
SHIPMENT TYPE GLOSSARY

FTL (Full Truckload)
  A shipment that fills an entire truck
  Typically 20,000 to 45,000 lbs or more
  Cost: Flat rate per load regardless of weight
  Use when: Volume justifies dedicated truck; 
            time-sensitive; fragile cargo

LTL (Less Than Truckload)
  A shipment that shares truck space with other shippers
  Typically under 10,000 lbs
  Cost: Based on weight; distance; and freight class
  Use when: Volume does not justify full truck; 
            cost efficiency prioritized over speed

AIR FREIGHT
  Fastest transit; highest cost per pound
  Use when: Urgency outweighs cost; 
            high-value low-weight goods
  Lead time advantage: Days vs weeks for ocean

OCEAN FREIGHT
  Slowest transit; lowest cost per unit for large volume
  FCL (Full Container Load) vs LCL (Less Container Load)
  Use when: Volume is large; timeline allows; 
            cost efficiency is priority

LAST MILE DELIVERY
  Final leg of delivery to end destination
  Most expensive segment per unit in the supply chain
  Key metrics: First attempt success rate; 
               time from hub to door

INTERMODAL
  Uses multiple transportation modes in a single shipment
  Example: Truck to rail to truck
  Balances cost and speed between pure truck and rail
```

## MODULE 2: Inbound and Outbound Operations

### 2.1 Inbound Shipment Processing

Inbound accuracy is the foundation of everything 
that comes after it. An error in receiving 
creates inventory discrepancies that ripple 
forward through fulfillment, customer service, 
and financial reporting.
```
INBOUND SHIPMENT PROCESSING PROCEDURE

PRE-ARRIVAL
☐ Confirm advance shipping notice (ASN) received 
  from vendor at least 24 hours before arrival
☐ Verify expected quantity; SKUs; and delivery window 
  against purchase order
☐ Assign dock door and receiving slot in system
☐ Notify warehouse team of incoming volume and type
☐ Flag any special handling requirements 
  (temperature; fragile; oversized)

RECEIPT AND INSPECTION
☐ Verify driver and carrier match booking record
☐ Inspect external condition of shipment before unloading
☐ Document any visible damage before signing 
  proof of delivery; never sign clean for a 
  damaged shipment
☐ Count cases or units against delivery manifest
☐ Note any discrepancies between manifest 
  and actual receipt immediately

SYSTEM PROCESSING
☐ Enter receipt into inventory management system 
  within 2 hours of unloading
☐ Reconcile actual receipt against purchase order
☐ Flag and document any discrepancies 
  (shorts; overages; wrong items)
☐ Generate receipt confirmation and distribute 
  to procurement and finance
☐ Update dock-to-stock timestamp in tracking system

EXCEPTION HANDLING
Any discrepancy between expected and received 
quantity greater than 2%: notify procurement 
and vendor within same business day.
Any damage discovered: photograph; document; 
notify vendor and carrier; hold for claims processing.
```

### 2.2 Outbound Shipment Processing

Outbound accuracy is where the customer's 
experience is either protected or damaged. 
Every step in this process exists because 
an error at any point becomes the customer's 
problem at the end.
```
OUTBOUND SHIPMENT PROCESSING PROCEDURE

ORDER VERIFICATION
☐ Confirm order details against system record
☐ Verify customer delivery address is complete 
  and accurate; address errors are one of the 
  top causes of failed deliveries
☐ Confirm special delivery instructions are 
  noted and communicated to carrier
☐ Verify shipment contents against order pick list
☐ Confirm appropriate carrier selected for 
  service level required

DOCUMENTATION
☐ Generate bill of lading (BOL) with accurate:
    Shipper and consignee information
    Commodity description and freight class
    Weight (actual and dimensional)
    Number of pieces and packaging type
    Any hazmat declarations if applicable
☐ Confirm carrier has copy of all required documents
☐ Record tracking number in system before shipment leaves

DISPATCH
☐ Confirm pickup window with carrier 
  at least 2 hours before scheduled time
☐ Obtain signed carrier receipt at pickup
☐ Enter dispatch confirmation in tracking system
☐ Set Zapier alert trigger for shipment monitoring
☐ Notify customer of dispatch with tracking 
  information if required by service agreement

POST-DISPATCH MONITORING
☐ Verify first tracking scan within 4 hours of pickup
☐ If no scan within 4 hours; contact carrier directly
☐ Monitor for exceptions per escalation procedure 
  in Module 4
```

### 2.3 Documentation Accuracy Standards
```
DOCUMENTATION: WHY EVERY FIELD MATTERS

Bill of Lading (BOL) errors cause:
  Customs delays for international shipments
  Delivery refusals by consignee
  Claims disputes with carriers
  Billing errors from carriers
  Audit findings in compliance reviews

The documentation accuracy target is 100%.
Not 98%. Not 99.5%. 100%.

Every field on a shipping document exists 
for a reason. If you are unsure what a field 
requires; ask before submitting; not after 
a problem occurs.

COMMON DOCUMENTATION ERRORS AND CONSEQUENCES

Wrong weight listed:
  Consequence: Incorrect freight charges; 
  potential carrier disputes; safety issues for overweight loads

Incomplete consignee address:
  Consequence: Delivery failure; 
  return freight cost; customer dissatisfaction

Wrong freight class:
  Consequence: Billing adjustment from carrier; 
  sometimes significantly higher than quoted rate

Missing special instructions:
  Consequence: Delivery attempt at wrong time; 
  failed first attempt delivery; 
  re-delivery cost and delay

Unsigned proof of delivery accepted for damaged goods:
  Consequence: Claim denial by carrier; 
  organization bears full cost of damaged shipment
```

### 3.1 The Carrier Tier Framework

Not all carriers are equal and not all shipments 
require the same level of carrier capability. 
Understanding the tier framework allows logistics 
staff to match shipments to the right carrier 
consistently rather than defaulting to whoever 
has capacity.
```
CARRIER TIER STRUCTURE

TIER 1: PREFERRED CARRIERS
  Criteria:   LOG-001 scorecard ≥ 90; 
              long-term contract in place; 
              highest reliability and service standards
  Use for:    Time-critical shipments; 
              high-value goods; key customer deliveries
  Examples:   [Organization's Tier 1 carriers listed here]

TIER 2: APPROVED CARRIERS
  Criteria:   LOG-001 scorecard 75 to 89; 
              active contract; meets core service standards
  Use for:    Standard shipments where Tier 1 
              is unavailable or at capacity
  Examples:   [Organization's Tier 2 carriers listed here]

TIER 3: BACKUP CARRIERS
  Criteria:   Vetted and approved; 
              no active volume commitment; 
              LOG-001 scorecard on file
  Use for:    Overflow capacity; 
              when Tier 1 and 2 are unavailable; 
              specific regional coverage gaps
  Activation: Requires Operations Manager approval
  Examples:   [Organization's Tier 3 carriers listed here]

UNAPPROVED CARRIERS
  Not on approved list: DO NOT USE

  Emergency carrier approval requires Operations Manager

### 3.3 Carrier Scorecard Contribution

Every logistics coordinator contributes to 
carrier performance data that feeds the quarterly 
scorecard in LOG-001. The accuracy of that 
scorecard depends on accurate daily documentation.
```
WHAT YOU ARE RESPONSIBLE FOR DOCUMENTING

Every exception:
  Date and time identified
  Shipment number and carrier
  Nature of exception (delay; damage; communication failure)
  Carrier response time and quality
  Resolution outcome and timeline

Every proactive notification:
  Did the carrier contact us before we contacted them?
  Document yes or no for every exception; 
  this feeds Category 2 of the scorecard directly

Every documentation error:
  Log in the documentation accuracy tracker
  Note which carrier; which document type; 
  what the error was

Every claims experience:
  Date filed; amount; resolution time; outcome

Your documentation is the data. 
Without it; the scorecard is an opinion. 
With it; the scorecard is a management tool.

## MODULE 4: Exception Handling and Escalation

### 4.1 Exception Classification Framework
```
SHIPMENT EXCEPTION PRIORITY LEVELS

P1: CRITICAL EXCEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Definition: Shipment failure with immediate significant 
            business impact

Triggers:
  Lost shipment with no location confirmation
  Active cargo theft or security breach
  Hazmat incident or safety emergency
  Complete carrier system failure affecting 
  multiple critical shipments
  Customs hold on time-critical international shipment

Response Time:   IMMEDIATE; within 15 minutes of identification
Escalation:      Logistics Coordinator → Operations Manager → 
                 Executive Leadership within 30 minutes
Communication:   Affected stakeholders notified within 1 hour

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

P2: HIGH PRIORITY EXCEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Definition: Significant delay or issue with confirmed 
            customer or operational impact

Triggers:
  Delivery will miss committed date by more than 24 hours
  Shipment damage confirmed
  Carrier has lost tracking visibility for 12+ hours
  Critical component shipment delayed affecting production

Response Time:   Within 1 hour of identification
Escalation:      Logistics Coordinator → Operations Manager 
                 within 2 hours if unresolved
Communication:   Affected stakeholders notified within 2 hours

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

P3: STANDARD EXCEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Definition: Delay or issue with manageable impact 
            and resolution path identified

Triggers:
  Delivery will miss committed date by less than 24 hours
  Minor damage with no functional impact
  Address correction required; delivery rescheduled
  Weather delay with confirmed new delivery date

Response Time:   Within 4 hours of identification
Escalation:      Operations Manager notification 
                 if unresolved within 8 hours
Communication:   Stakeholder notification same business day

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

P4: INFORMATIONAL EXCEPTION
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Definition: Minor deviation with no delivery impact

Triggers:
  Scan gap of less than 8 hours subsequently resolved
  Minor transit time variance within acceptable range
  Delivery window shifted within same day

Response Time:   Monitor and document
Escalation:      Not required unless escalates to P3
Communication:   Include in daily summary report
```

### 4.2 Exception Response Protocol
```
EXCEPTION RESPONSE STEP BY STEP

STEP 1: IDENTIFY AND CLASSIFY
  Identify the exception from tracking system; 
  carrier alert; or Zapier notification
  Classify as P1; P2; P3; or P4 using 
  the framework above
  Do not downgrade classification to avoid escalation; 
  if in doubt classify higher and adjust down 
  when more information is available

STEP 2: CONTACT CARRIER
  Contact carrier account manager or operations 
  line immediately for P1 and P2
  For P1: Phone call required; do not use email alone
  Obtain: Current location of shipment; 
          root cause of delay; 
          revised estimated delivery date; 
          what carrier is doing to resolve
  Document the call: who you spoke to; 
  what they said; what was committed

STEP 3: NOTIFY STAKEHOLDERS
  Notify affected internal stakeholders 
  per response timeline for exception level
  Lead with what you know; not what you do not know
  Provide estimated resolution timeline 
  even if uncertain; update when information improves
  Never let a stakeholder find out about 
  an exception from someone other than logistics

STEP 4: DOCUMENT AND TRACK
  Enter exception in Notion exception log immediately
  Update every 2 hours for P1; 
  every 4 hours for P2 until resolved
  Do not close the exception until delivery confirmed

STEP 5: RESOLVE AND CLOSE
  Confirm delivery or resolution with documentation
  Close exception in tracking system with 
  final disposition notes
  Flag for inclusion in carrier scorecard 
  data for the quarter
  Identify root cause for recurring exception patterns
```

### 4.3 Root Cause Analysis for Recurring Exceptions
```
WHEN TO CONDUCT ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS

Trigger a root cause review when:
  Same carrier has 3 or more exceptions 
  of same type in a 30-day period
  Single exception results in significant 
  customer impact or financial loss
  Exception reveals a process gap 
  rather than a carrier performance issue

ROOT CAUSE FRAMEWORK: THE 5 WHYS

Start with the problem statement and ask 
"why" five times to get past symptoms 
to the underlying cause.

Example:
Problem: Shipment delivered 2 days late

Why 1: Carrier missed pickup window
Why 2: Carrier was not notified of pickup 
       until same day as scheduled time
Why 3: Order was not processed until 
       late afternoon day before pickup
Why 4: Order approval process requires 
       two signatures and second approver 
       was traveling
Why 5: No backup approval authority 
       defined for approver absences

Root Cause: Process gap; no backup approval authority
Solution:   Define and communicate backup approval 
            authority for all order approvers
Owner:      Operations Manager
Timeline:   Implemented within 30 days
```

### 5.2 Zapier Alert System for Logistics Staff

The Zapier automation system described in 
AUTOMATION-002 runs in the background and 
routes exception alerts to the right people 
automatically. Understanding what it does 
and what it does not do is important for 
every logistics coordinator.
```
WHAT ZAPIER DOES AUTOMATICALLY

Monitors Google Sheets tracking dashboard 
for status changes every 15 minutes

When "Delayed" status detected:
  Sends Slack alert to logistics channel
  Sends email notification to logistics coordinator
  Creates task in Notion exception log
  Escalates to Operations Manager if P1 or P2

What Zapier DOES NOT do:
  Call the carrier for you
  Resolve the exception
  Notify the customer
  Use judgment about severity

Zapier is an alert system; not a response system.
When you receive a Zapier alert; 
your job has just started; not finished.
A Zapier alert is the same as a colleague 
tapping you on the shoulder and saying 
"this needs your attention right now."
```

### 6.2 Physical Security in Logistics Operations
```
CARGO SECURITY PROTOCOL

SHIPMENT BOOKING SECURITY
  Verify carrier identity before booking; 
  do not book with carriers not on the approved list
  Confirm driver and vehicle information 
  before pickup on high-value shipments
  Do not share detailed shipment contents 
  publicly or in unsecured communications
  Use generic commodity descriptions on 
  public-facing documents where possible 
  for high-value goods

AT PICKUP AND DELIVERY
  Verify driver ID against booking record for 
  high-value shipments
  Do not release shipment to unverified driver 
  regardless of urgency claimed
  Document any discrepancy between expected 
  and actual pickup personnel immediately
  Ensure seal numbers are recorded and verified 
  at origin and confirmed at destination

WAREHOUSE AND DOCK SECURITY
  Visitors and carriers do not have unsupervised 
  access to warehouse areas
  Dock doors remain closed when not in active use
  Verify identity of all personnel requesting 
  access to shipping and receiving areas
  Report any unfamiliar vehicles conducting 
  surveillance of facility to Operations Manager

HIGH-VALUE SHIPMENT PROTOCOL
  High-value threshold: [Organization defined]
  Additional steps required:
    GPS tracking activated before departure
    Direct carrier contact every 4 hours in transit
    Signed sealed trailer verification at origin
    Seal verification required at destination 
    before acceptance
```

### 6.3 Cybersecurity Threats in Logistics Operations
```
CYBER THREATS SPECIFIC TO LOGISTICS ROLES

FREIGHT FRAUD AND DOUBLE BROKERING
  What it is: Fraudulent carrier accepts shipment 
              booking; often re-brokers to unknown 
              carrier; collects payment without 
              performing service; or steals cargo
  How to prevent: 
    Book only with carriers on the approved list
    Verify carrier MC and DOT numbers before booking
    Never book through unsolicited carriers 
    who contact you offering capacity

SHIPMENT DIVERSION FRAUD
  What it is: Attacker intercepts communication 
              and diverts shipment to unauthorized location
  How to prevent:
    Verify any address change requests through 
    separate communication channel
    Do not process address changes received 
    only via email without phone verification

EMAIL-BASED CARRIER FRAUD
  What it is: Fraudulent email impersonating 
              a known carrier requesting payment 
              detail updates or delivery changes
  How to prevent:
    Verify payment change requests by calling 
    the carrier on their known number
    Never update payment details based on 
    email instruction alone

LOGISTICS SYSTEM PHISHING
  What it is: Phishing email designed to look 
              like a TMS notification; 
              carrier portal alert; or 
              tracking system message
  How to prevent:
    Access tracking systems by typing 
    the URL directly; never from email links
    Report suspicious emails to IT Security 
    before clicking anything

REPORTING SUPPLY CHAIN SECURITY CONCERNS
  Physical security concern: Operations Manager immediately
  Cybersecurity concern: IT Security immediately 
  and Operations Manager simultaneously
  Active cargo theft: Law enforcement and 
  Operations Manager simultaneously; 
  preserve all evidence and documentation
```

Logistics coordinators are the operational 
heartbeat of the supply chain. You make the

Shipment lifecycle management
  You own every shipment from booking to 
  delivery confirmation. Nothing falls through 
  the cracks because you actively monitor; 
  not because you wait for alerts.

Carrier relationship management
  You are the day-to-day contact for carrier 
  account managers. The quality of those 
  relationships directly affects how carriers 
  prioritize your shipments when capacity is tight.

Exception ownership
  When an exception occurs; it is yours to resolve. 
  You contact the carrier; you notify stakeholders;

  to resolution. You do not pass it to someone else 
  and wait.

Data accuracy
  Every tracking update; every exception log; 
  every carrier scorecard data point flows 
  from your documentation. 
  If the data is not there; it did not happen.

### TRACK B: Warehouse and Receiving Staff

**Your Role in the Operation**

Warehouse and receiving staff are where the 
physical reality of logistics meets the data 
that drives every downstream decision. If what 
is physically in the warehouse does not match 
what the system says is there; nothing downstream 
works correctly. Your accuracy is the foundation.
```
CORE COMPETENCIES FOR WAREHOUSE AND RECEIVING STAFF

Inbound verification accuracy
  Count; verify; and document every inbound receipt 
  against the manifest. Discrepancies found at receipt 
  are easy to resolve. Discrepancies discovered 
  three weeks later during inventory reconciliation 
  cost significantly more to investigate and resolve.

Damage documentation
  Document damage before signing anything. 
  A signed clean proof of delivery for a 
  damaged shipment removes your organization's 
  ability to recover costs from the carrier. 
  Photograph; document; and note on the 
  delivery receipt every time; no exceptions.

System update speed
  Dock-to-stock time starts when a truck arrives 
  and ends when inventory is in the system and available. 
  Your speed in processing receipts directly 
  affects the organization's ability to fulfill 
  outbound orders.

Physical security enforcement
  You are on the front line of physical supply 
  chain security. Verify driver identity on 
  high-value shipments. Report unfamiliar 
  personnel or vehicles immediately. 
  Never allow unsupervised dock access.

DAILY TOOLS
TMS receiving module for receipt processing; 
Inventory management system for stock updates; 
Google Sheets for discrepancy logging; 
Smartphone or tablet for damage photography; 
Notion for exception and damage documentation
```

### TRACK C: Operations Managers

**Your Role in the Operation**

Operations managers set the standards; 
build the systems; and make the decisions 
that determine whether the logistics operation 
performs or underperforms. Your job is not 
to manage every shipment; it is to build the 
team; tools; and processes that manage shipments 
reliably without requiring your daily intervention.
```
CORE COMPETENCIES FOR OPERATIONS MANAGERS

Performance architecture
  Define the KPIs; set the targets; 
  build the dashboards that make performance 
  visible to everyone including yourself. 
  You cannot manage what is not measured 
  and your team cannot improve toward 
  a target they cannot see.

Carrier relationship governance
  You own the strategic carrier relationships. 
  Conduct quarterly business reviews using LOG-001. 
  Make the decisions about tier placement; 
  volume allocation; and contract renewal 
  based on performance data; not history or comfort.

Escalation resolution
  P1 exceptions escalate to you. 
  Your response sets the standard for 
  how the team approaches urgency. 
  Every P1 resolution should produce 
  a root cause and a process improvement.

Risk management ownership
  LOG-002 is your document. Review it bi-annually. 
  Test the contingency plans. Ensure backup 
  carrier relationships are current and activated. 
  Do not wait for a disruption to discover 
  that the contingency plan has not been maintained.

Security governance
  You approve all new carrier relationships. 
  You receive security incident escalations first. 
  You are responsible for ensuring the team 
  knows and follows supply chain security protocols.

MANAGEMENT TOOLS
DASHBOARD-001 for operational KPI visibility; 
LOG-001 for carrier performance governance; 
LOG-002 for risk and contingency management; 
SOP-002 for process standards; 
Notion for team and project management; 
Gamma and PowerPoint for leadership reporting
```

### TRACK D: Customer Service and Fulfillment Staff

**Your Role in the Operation**

Customer service and fulfillment staff are 
the bridge between the logistics operation 
and the customer's experience. You translate 
operational reality into customer communication 
and you are often the first to know when something 
has gone wrong because the customer tells you 
before the logistics system does.
```
CORE COMPETENCIES FOR CUSTOMER SERVICE 
AND FULFILLMENT STAFF

Order accuracy
  Fulfillment accuracy starts with order entry. 
  Verify order details before submission. 
  A wrong SKU entered confidently is more 
  expensive than a question asked upfront.

Proactive customer communication
  When logistics tells you a shipment is delayed; 
  contact the customer before they contact you. 
  Proactive communication preserves relationships 
  that reactive communication damages.

Escalation judgment
  You need to know the difference between a 
  customer complaint that logistics can resolve 
  and one that requires management involvement. 
  Escalate to logistics when a shipment issue 
  requires carrier intervention. 
  Escalate to Operations Manager when a customer 
  relationship is at risk.

Accurate exception reporting to logistics
  When a customer reports a delivery issue 
  you are not already aware of; that is new 
  information for the logistics team. 
  Report it immediately with full details: 
  order number; what the customer reported; 
  when they reported it; what you told them.

DAILY TOOLS
Order management system for order entry and tracking; 
TMS tracking visibility for shipment status; 
Notion for customer issue documentation; 
ChatGPT for professional customer 
communication drafting; 
Gmail for customer correspondence
```
