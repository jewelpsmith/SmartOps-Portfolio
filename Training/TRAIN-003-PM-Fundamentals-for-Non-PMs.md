## 📊 Why Project Management Fundamentals Matter: The Data

You do not need a PMP certification to work 
effectively on a project team. But you do need 
to understand how projects work; why they fail; 
and what your role is in making them succeed. 
The data makes the stakes clear.
```
PROJECT MANAGEMENT BY THE NUMBERS

70%    of projects fail to meet their original
       scope, schedule, or budget goals
       Source: PMI Pulse of the Profession 2023

$122M  is wasted for every $1 billion invested
       in projects due to poor project performance
       Source: PMI 2023

Only   35% of projects are completed on time 
       and within budget
       Source: Standish Group CHAOS Report 2023

89%    of high-performing organizations value
       project management; compared to 36% of 
       low performers
       Source: PMI 2023

Organizations with mature PM practices waste 
28 times less money than those without
       Source: PMI Pulse of the Profession 2023

Unclear requirements cause 37% of project failures
       Source: PMI 2023

Poor communication causes 29% of project failures
       Source: PMI 2023

Lack of resource planning causes 18% of failures
       Source: PMI 2023
```

The most important insight in this data is that 
the top two causes of project failure; unclear 
requirements and poor communication; are not 
technical problems. They are human problems that 
every person on the project team has the ability 
to prevent regardless of their title or role.

This training exists to give you the foundation 
to be a genuinely valuable team member on any 
project; not just someone who attends the meetings.

## MODULE 1: How Projects Actually Work

### 1.1 What Makes Something a Project

Not everything that needs to get done is a project. 
Understanding the difference between ongoing 
operations and a project shapes how work is 
planned, resourced, and managed.
```
PROJECT vs OPERATIONS

A PROJECT is:
  Temporary; has a defined start and end date
  Unique; produces a specific output or outcome
  Cross-functional; typically involves people 
  from multiple teams or disciplines
  Constrained; operating within defined scope; 
  schedule; and budget

Examples of projects:
  Implementing a new logistics software platform
  Onboarding a new major vendor relationship
  Developing and launching a new employee training program
  Responding to and remediating a security incident

OPERATIONS are:
  Ongoing; recurring activities with no defined end
  Repeatable; following established processes and SOPs
  Functional; typically within a single team or department
  
Examples of operations:
  Processing daily shipments
  Running weekly status reports
  Onboarding individual new hires per SOP-001
  Maintaining the vendor scorecard per LOG-001
```

### 1.2 The Project Lifecycle

Every project; regardless of methodology; moves 
through a lifecycle. Understanding where a project 
is in its lifecycle tells you what kinds of work 
are happening and what your team needs from you.
```
THE FIVE PROJECT PHASES

INITIATING
What happens: Project is formally defined and authorized
Key outputs:  Project charter; stakeholder identification; 
              initial scope and objectives documented
Your role:    Provide input if asked; understand the 
              project purpose before work begins

PLANNING
What happens: Detailed plan developed; scope defined; 
              schedule built; resources assigned; 
              risks identified
Key outputs:  Project plan; work breakdown structure; 
              risk register; communication plan
Your role:    Confirm your availability; review and 
              validate your assigned scope; raise 
              concerns before work starts not after

EXECUTING
What happens: The actual project work gets done
Key outputs:  Deliverables; completed work packages; 
              status reports; issue logs
Your role:    Complete your tasks on time; communicate 
              blockers early; attend required meetings; 
              update your task status consistently

MONITORING AND CONTROLLING
What happens: Progress tracked against plan; 
              changes managed; risks monitored
Key outputs:  Status reports; change requests; 
              updated risk register
Your role:    Provide accurate status updates; 
              flag risks you observe; do not hide problems

CLOSING
What happens: Project formally closed; lessons learned 
              documented; team recognized
Key outputs:  Final deliverable acceptance; 
              lessons learned document; team retrospective
Your role:    Complete final assigned tasks; 
              participate in retrospective honestly; 
              transition knowledge to operations team
```

### 1.3 The Triple Constraint

Every project operates within three constraints 
that are always in tension with each other. 
Understanding this triangle helps you understand 
why your project manager sometimes has to make 
decisions that seem frustrating from your vantage point.
```
THE PROJECT MANAGEMENT TRIPLE CONSTRAINT

         SCOPE
           △
          / \
         /   \
        /     \
       /  QUALITY\
      ─────────────
   TIME         COST

SCOPE:  What the project will deliver
TIME:   When it will be delivered
COST:   What resources are available

THE IRON LAW:
You can have it good; fast; or cheap.
Pick two.

If scope increases and timeline cannot move; 
cost must increase (more resources needed).

If timeline is compressed and budget is fixed; 
scope must decrease (less gets delivered).

If budget is cut and timeline is fixed; 
scope must decrease or quality will suffer.

WHY THIS MATTERS TO YOU:
When a project manager says we cannot add that 
to this project; they are managing the triple 
constraint on your behalf. The answer is 
usually not no; it is not in this project.
```

## MODULE 2: Waterfall vs Agile

### 2.1 Waterfall Project Management

Waterfall is the traditional sequential approach 
to project management. Each phase must be completed 
before the next begins; like water flowing down 
a series of steps.
```
HOW WATERFALL WORKS

REQUIREMENTS → DESIGN → BUILD → TEST → DEPLOY → MAINTAIN

Each phase is fully completed before moving forward.
Changes to requirements after planning are expensive 
and disruptive.
Detailed documentation is produced at each phase.
The full deliverable is revealed at the end of the project.

WHEN WATERFALL WORKS WELL
  Requirements are well understood and unlikely to change
  Regulatory or compliance requirements demand 
  detailed documentation at each stage
  The cost of change is very high (construction; 
  manufacturing; government contracts)
  External vendors or contractors need detailed 
  specifications upfront

WHAT WATERFALL LOOKS LIKE IN PRACTICE FOR YOU
  You will be given detailed requirements early
  Your deliverables will have fixed scope and deadlines
  Changes go through a formal change control process
  Status is reported against a detailed project plan
  Your project manager has a Gantt chart
```

### 2.2 Agile Project Management

Agile is an iterative approach that delivers 
work in short cycles called sprints, typically 
1 to 2 weeks. Each sprint produces a working 
increment of the final deliverable.
```
HOW AGILE WORKS

Sprint 1 → Review → Sprint 2 → Review → Sprint 3 → Review → Release
(Plan)(Build)(Test)  (Plan)(Build)(Test)

Work is broken into small deliverable chunks.
Priorities can shift between sprints based on feedback.
The team self-organizes around the work.
Working software or deliverables are produced early.
Stakeholder feedback is built into the process.

AGILE VOCABULARY YOU NEED TO KNOW

Sprint:         A time-boxed work cycle; usually 1 to 2 weeks
Backlog:        The full list of work to be done; 
                prioritized by importance
Sprint Backlog: The specific work items selected 
                for a given sprint
Story Points:   A measure of effort; not time
Velocity:       How many story points the team 
                completes per sprint on average
Stand-up:       Daily 15-minute team sync; 
                what did I do; what am I doing; 
                what is blocking me
Retrospective:  End-of-sprint review of how the 
                team worked together; not what was built
Scrum Master:   Facilitator who removes blockers 
                and protects the team's process
Product Owner:  Defines and prioritizes the backlog; 
                represents the business or customer

WHAT AGILE LOOKS LIKE IN PRACTICE FOR YOU
  Your work is organized in 1 to 2 week sprints
  You attend a daily stand-up; keep it to 3 sentences
  Your tasks live in Jira with sprint assignments
  Sprint planning happens at the start of each cycle
  You demo completed work at sprint review
  Retrospectives are safe spaces; speak honestly
```

### 2.3 Choosing the Right Approach
```
WATERFALL OR AGILE? A QUICK DECISION GUIDE

USE WATERFALL WHEN:
  Requirements are fixed and fully understood
  Regulatory documentation requirements are heavy
  The team is distributed or works across time zones
  External contracts require detailed specifications
  Changes would be very expensive

USE AGILE WHEN:
  Requirements may evolve as work progresses
  Fast feedback loops are valuable
  The team is co-located or in aligned time zones
  Innovation and iteration are expected
  Getting something working early has business value

HYBRID:
  Many organizations use a hybrid approach; 
  planning is structured like Waterfall while 
  execution uses Agile sprints
  This is increasingly common in operations 
  and logistics environments
```

## MODULE 3: Your Role on a Project Team

### 3.1 Common Project Roles

Understanding the roles around you helps you 
know who to go to for what and what each 
person needs from you to do their job effectively.
```
PROJECT TEAM ROLES AND WHAT THEY NEED FROM YOU

PROJECT MANAGER
  Their job:  Plan; coordinate; track; report; 
              manage risks; remove blockers; 
              keep the project on track
  They need from you:
    Accurate and timely status updates
    Early warning when you are going to miss a deadline
    Honest assessment of task complexity
    Attendance at required meetings
    Escalation of blockers before they cause delays

PRODUCT OWNER / BUSINESS SPONSOR
  Their job:  Define what success looks like; 
              prioritize requirements; make 
              scope decisions; approve deliverables
  They need from you:
    Clear questions when requirements are ambiguous
    Input on feasibility and effort from your domain
    Demonstrations of completed work
    Honest feedback on whether requirements are achievable

SCRUM MASTER (Agile teams)
  Their job:  Facilitate Agile ceremonies; remove 
              blockers; protect the team's process
  They need from you:
    Daily stand-up attendance and participation
    Honest communication about blockers
    Respect for the Agile process even when it feels slow

SUBJECT MATTER EXPERTS AND CONTRIBUTORS
  That is you. Your job is covered in section 3.2.

STAKEHOLDERS
  Their job:  Provide requirements; review deliverables; 
              approve changes; fund the project
  They need from you:
    Work that meets the requirements they defined
    Communication when you identify risks they 
    need to be aware of
```

### 3.2 Your Responsibilities as a Contributor

This is the section that most non-PM training 
skips. Being a good project contributor is a 
skill; and it is one that project managers 
notice and remember.
```
THE FIVE COMMITMENTS OF A STRONG CONTRIBUTOR

1. OWN YOUR TASKS
   You are accountable for your assigned work.
   Not your manager. Not the project manager.
   You.
   If you are going to miss a deadline; 
   that is information the team needs 
   as early as possible; not on the due date.

2. UPDATE YOUR STATUS HONESTLY
   A task marked In Progress for two weeks 
   with no update is a risk no one can see.
   Update your task status in Jira or Notion 
   at least every 2 business days.
   If something is stuck; mark it Blocked 
   and explain why. Do not leave it In Progress 
   hoping it will unstick itself.

3. RAISE RISKS EARLY
   If you see something that could affect the 
   project; a dependency that is not ready; 
   a requirement that seems unclear; a timeline 
   that seems unrealistic; raise it immediately.
   The project manager cannot manage risks 
   they do not know about.
   There is no bad time to raise a risk. 
   There is only a worse time; which is later.

4. ASK QUESTIONS BEFORE STARTING
   Spending 3 days building the wrong thing 
   because you did not clarify the requirement 
   is one of the most common and costly 
   project problems.
   Ask questions before you start.
   Confirm your understanding before you build.
   A 10-minute clarification conversation is 
   worth 3 days of rework every single time.

5. COMMUNICATE COMPLETIONS CLEARLY
   When a task is done; say so.
   Mark it complete in the project tool.
   Notify whoever needs to know.
   Attach the deliverable or link to it.
   Do not assume that being done is obvious to anyone else.
```

## MODULE 4: Tools Walkthrough

### 4.1 Notion for Project Contributors
```
NOTION NAVIGATION GUIDE FOR CONTRIBUTORS

FINDING YOUR WORK
  Projects are organized in the team workspace
  Your assigned tasks appear in the project database
  Filter by "Assigned To: Me" to see your work
  Status options: Not Started; In Progress; 
                  Blocked; In Review; Complete

UPDATING YOUR TASKS
  Click on a task to open the detail view
  Update the Status field first
  Add a comment describing what you did or 
  what is blocking you
  Link to any relevant files or documents
  Tag your project manager if action is needed from them

FINDING DOCUMENTS AND SOPS
  Project documents live in the project's 
  Document section
  Organization-wide SOPs are in the SOP Library
  Use the search bar at the top for anything 
  you cannot find by browsing

COMMUNICATION IN NOTION
  @mention a person to notify them directly
  @mention a page to link to a document
  Comments on tasks are the preferred way 
  to discuss task-specific questions
  Use Slack for real-time conversation; 
  use Notion for documented decisions

DAILY HABIT
  Open Notion at the start of every workday
  Review your assigned tasks
  Update any status that has changed
  Check for comments or @mentions from teammates
```

### 4.2 Jira for Project Contributors
```
JIRA NAVIGATION GUIDE FOR CONTRIBUTORS

YOUR BOARD VIEW
  The board shows all work in the current sprint
  Columns: To Do → In Progress → In Review → Done
  Your assigned issues appear with your avatar
  Drag issues across columns as status changes

UNDERSTANDING YOUR ISSUES
  Each issue has a type: Story; Task; Bug; Sub-task
  Story Points show estimated effort; not hours
  Priority: Highest; High; Medium; Low; Lowest
  Sprint field shows which sprint the work belongs to

UPDATING AN ISSUE
  Click the issue to open detail view
  Change the status using the workflow button at top
  Log time if your team tracks time in Jira
  Add a comment for any important update or decision
  Attach files or link to external documents

THE DAILY STAND-UP IN JIRA
  Before stand-up; review your active issues
  Know the answer to these three questions:
    What did I complete since last stand-up?
    What am I working on today?
    Is anything blocking me?
  If something is blocked; add the Blocked label 
  and comment explaining the blocker

SPRINT CEREMONIES AND YOUR ROLE
  Sprint Planning: Review upcoming issues; 
    confirm you understand your assignments; 
    raise concerns about scope or timeline
  Sprint Review: Demo or describe your completed work; 
    be specific about what you built and why it matters
  Retrospective: Share one thing that went well 
    and one thing to improve; be honest and constructive
```

## MODULE 5: Communication on a Project Team

### 5.1 Communication That Protects the Project
```
THE COMMUNICATION RULE EVERY CONTRIBUTOR 
NEEDS TO INTERNALIZE:

Your project manager cannot manage information 
they do not have.

Every time you discover a risk; encounter a blocker; 
realize a timeline is unrealistic; or identify 
a dependency that is not accounted for; 
you have a choice.

You can say something now.
Or you can hope it works out and say something later.

The cost of saying something now is a short conversation.
The cost of saying something later is paid by 
the entire team and everyone depending on the project.

Say something now. Every time. Without exception.
```

### 5.2 Status Update Framework

A strong status update has three components. 
This framework applies whether you are updating 
a task in Jira, posting in Slack, or giving 
a verbal update in a stand-up.
```
THE PROGRESS; PLAN; PROBLEM FRAMEWORK

PROGRESS:   What have you completed since the last update?
            Be specific. Name the deliverable or task. 
            State that it is done if it is done.

PLAN:       What are you working on next?
            Be specific. Name the task. 
            Give an estimated completion if you can.

PROBLEM:    Is anything blocking you or creating risk?
            If yes; name it clearly and say 
            what you need to unblock.
            If no; say "no blockers" explicitly 
            so the team knows you considered it.

EXAMPLE STAND-UP USING THIS FRAMEWORK

Weak version:
"I am still working on the vendor assessment. 
It is going fine."

Strong version:
"I completed the data gathering for vendors A 
and B yesterday and submitted for review. 
Today I am starting the assessment for vendor C 
and expect to have the first draft done by end of day. 
One issue: vendor D has not responded to my 
information request in 5 days; I need a decision 
on whether to escalate or proceed without their data."

The strong version takes 20 more seconds to say 
and gives the project manager everything they 
need to protect the project.
```

### 5.3 Escalation; When and How
```
ESCALATION IS NOT FAILURE

One of the most common mistakes project contributors 
make is waiting too long to escalate a problem 
because they want to solve it themselves first 
or because they do not want to seem like they 
cannot handle their work.

Escalating promptly when you identify a risk 
or blocker is one of the most valued behaviors 
on a project team. It is how mature, experienced 
professionals operate.

WHEN TO ESCALATE IMMEDIATELY
  A task dependency is not ready and will 
  delay your deliverable by more than 1 day
  A requirement is unclear and the person 
  who can clarify is not responding
  You have discovered a risk the project 
  manager is not aware of
  Your estimated timeline has changed materially
  A decision needs to be made that is above 
  your authority or outside your scope

HOW TO ESCALATE EFFECTIVELY
  Be specific: name the issue; the impact; 
  and the decision or action you need
  Give options where you can: here are three 
  ways we could resolve this
  State the timeline: this needs a decision 
  by [date] to avoid impacting the schedule
  Do not just report a problem; bring context 
  and a recommended path forward when possible
```

## MODULE 6: AI Tools in Project Work

### 6.1 AI Tools That Improve Project Contributions

AI tools are not replacing project management. 
They are accelerating specific parts of the work 
when used thoughtfully. As a contributor; 
knowing which tools to use for which tasks 
makes you meaningfully more productive.
```
AI TOOL APPLICATIONS FOR PROJECT CONTRIBUTORS

DRAFTING AND WRITING
  Tool:    ChatGPT; Notion AI
  Use for: Drafting status updates; meeting notes; 
           requirement documentation; email communication
  Prompt example: "Draft a project status update 
  summarizing the following progress: [paste your notes]. 
  Audience is the project sponsor. Keep it under 150 words."

RESEARCH AND ANALYSIS
  Tool:    Perplexity
  Use for: Research on industry standards; 
           competitor analysis; technical background; 
           regulatory requirements
  Prompt example: "What are the current best practices 
  for vendor onboarding security assessments 
  in logistics organizations? Cite sources."

PRESENTATIONS AND VISUALS
  Tool:    Gamma; Canva
  Use for: Sprint review presentations; 
           stakeholder update decks; 
           process flow visuals
  Prompt example: "Create a 5-slide presentation 
  summarizing a sprint review for a logistics 
  platform implementation project."

MEETING PREPARATION
  Tool:    ChatGPT
  Use for: Preparing discussion questions; 
           agenda creation; pre-read summaries
  Prompt example: "I have a kickoff meeting for 
  a vendor management system implementation. 
  Generate 8 clarifying questions I should ask 
  to ensure requirements are fully understood."

RISK IDENTIFICATION
  Tool:    ChatGPT; Perplexity
  Use for: Brainstorming risks; reviewing 
           risk registers for completeness
  Prompt example: "What are the top 10 risks 
  for a logistics software implementation project 
  in a mid-size operations organization? 
  Categorize by likelihood and impact."
```

### 6.2 AI Tool Boundaries in Project Work
```
USE AI TOOLS FOR:
  Drafting; editing; and improving your writing
  Research and background information
  Brainstorming options; risks; and approaches
  Creating visual presentations from your content
  Summarizing long documents you need to read
  Generating questions before important meetings

DO NOT USE AI TOOLS FOR:
  Inputting confidential project data; 
  financial figures; or client information 
  into public AI tools
  Making project decisions on your behalf
  Generating final deliverables without 
  your review; editing; and validation
  Replacing conversations that need to happen 
  with real stakeholders

THE CORE PRINCIPLE:
AI tools accelerate your thinking. 
They do not replace it. 
Everything that comes out of an AI tool 
is a first draft that you are responsible 
for reviewing; validating; and owning.

If something is wrong in an AI-generated 
deliverable and you submitted it without 
reviewing it; the error is yours; not the tool's.
```

### TRACK A: Operations and Logistics Contributors

**Your Project Context**

Operations and logistics staff most commonly 
contribute to projects involving process 
improvement; vendor onboarding; system 
implementation; and logistics optimization. 
Your domain expertise is what makes these 
projects successful; the PM provides the 
structure around your knowledge.
```
WHAT YOUR PROJECT MANAGER NEEDS FROM YOU

Requirements clarity
  You know the operations better than anyone 
  in the room. When requirements are being defined; 
  speak up if something will not work in practice. 
  A requirement that looks good on a whiteboard 
  but cannot be executed operationally is not 
  a good requirement.

Process documentation
  Your ability to document operational processes 
  clearly and accurately is one of your highest-value 
  project contributions. Use SOP templates to 
  structure this work consistently.

Exception and edge case identification
  Operational processes have exceptions. 
  Projects that do not account for exceptions 
  fail during implementation. When reviewing 
  requirements; always ask: what happens when 
  this does not go to plan?

Testing and validation
  User acceptance testing for operational systems 
  requires people who do the work every day. 
  When asked to test; test thoroughly; 
  including edge cases and exception scenarios.

TOOLS FOR YOUR TRACK
Notion for task tracking and documentation; 
Jira if your project uses sprint management; 
Google Sheets for operational data and testing logs; 
Zapier if you are involved in automation projects;
ChatGPT for SOP drafting assistance
```

### TRACK B: IT and Security Contributors

**Your Project Context**

IT and security staff contribute to virtually 
every significant project as technical reviewers; 
architects; implementers; and security gatekeepers. 
Your role often spans multiple projects simultaneously 
which requires disciplined prioritization.
```
WHAT YOUR PROJECT MANAGER NEEDS FROM YOU

Technical feasibility assessment
  When requirements are being defined; validate 
  whether they are technically achievable within 
  the proposed timeline and budget. 
  The earlier you raise feasibility concerns; 
  the cheaper they are to address.

Security requirements input
  Every project should have security requirements 
  defined before design begins. Your job is to 
  ensure security is designed in; not bolted on 
  at the end when changes are expensive.

Dependency identification
  IT projects almost always have dependencies 
  on other systems; teams; or external vendors. 
  Mapping and communicating these dependencies 
  early is one of your most critical contributions.

Capacity and availability transparency
  If you are assigned to multiple projects; 
  your project managers need to know your 
  realistic availability. 
  Over-commitment leads to delays across 
  all your projects simultaneously.

TOOLS FOR YOUR TRACK
Jira for sprint-based project management; 
Notion for security documentation and runbooks; 
Perplexity for security research and threat intelligence; 
ChatGPT for security report drafting; 
Excel for risk registers and vulnerability tracking
```

### TRACK C: Administrative and Coordination Staff

**Your Project Context**

Administrative and coordination staff are the 
operational backbone of project execution. 
You keep things organized; documented; and moving 
when the complexity of a large project threatens to 
overwhelm the team's ability to track what is happening.
```
WHAT YOUR PROJECT MANAGER NEEDS FROM YOU

Meeting logistics and documentation
  Scheduling; agenda distribution; and meeting 
  notes are your domain. Good meeting notes 
  capture decisions; action items with owners; 
  and deadlines. They are reference documents; 
  not transcripts.

Action item tracking
  Action items without owners and deadlines 
  do not get done. Your role is to ensure 
  that every action item leaving a meeting 
  has a name; a deadline; and ends up in 
  the project tracker before end of day.

Document management
  Project documents have a lifecycle. 
  Drafts become reviewed versions become 
  approved final versions. Keeping the 
  document repository organized and current 
  is work that everyone benefits from 
  and almost no one does without being asked.

Stakeholder communication support
  Status reports; meeting invitations; 
  distribution lists; and stakeholder 
  communications often flow through 
  coordination staff. Accuracy and timeliness 
  in these communications directly affects 
  the project's credibility with stakeholders.

TOOLS FOR YOUR TRACK
Notion for document management and task tracking; 
Gmail and Google Calendar for scheduling; 
Canva for document formatting and visual aids; 
ChatGPT for drafting meeting notes and communications; 
Zapier for automating recurring communications
```

### TRACK D: Subject Matter Experts and Consultants

**Your Project Context**

SMEs and consultants are brought into projects 
for specific expertise. Your time is usually 
the scarcest resource on the project. Making 
that time as impactful as possible requires 
clear communication about what you need 
from the team and what the team needs from you.
```
WHAT YOUR PROJECT MANAGER NEEDS FROM YOU

Availability clarity upfront
  Be specific about when you are available and 
  for how long. A SME who is available 
  "as needed" but takes 3 days to respond 
  is a project risk. 
  Define your engagement terms early.

Documented knowledge transfer
  Your expertise cannot leave the project 
  when your engagement ends. Everything you 
  contribute should be documented well enough 
  that someone else can maintain and build on it.

Assumptions and constraints identification
  Your expertise means you can see assumptions 
  and constraints the team cannot. 
  Documenting these explicitly early protects

Scope boundary management
  SME scope creep happens when "quick questions" 
  accumulate into unplanned workload. 
  Define what is in and out of scope for 
  your engagement and communicate changes 
  to your project manager promptly.
```
