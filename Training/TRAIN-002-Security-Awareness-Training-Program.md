Operations and logistics organizations are

## MODULE 2: Phishing and Social Engineering

### 2.1 The Anatomy of a Phishing Attack

Phishing works because it exploits human psychology 
rather than technical vulnerabilities. Understanding 
the psychological mechanisms attackers use is 
the most effective defense.
```
THE SIX PSYCHOLOGICAL TRIGGERS ATTACKERS USE

1. URGENCY
   "Your account will be locked in 2 hours"
   "Immediate action required"
   "Final notice"
   
   Why it works: Urgency bypasses careful thinking 
   by activating a fight-or-flight response that 
   prioritizes fast action over deliberate analysis.

2. AUTHORITY
   "This is the CEO"
   "IT Security Department"
   "IRS Notice"
   
   Why it works: We are conditioned to respond 
   to authority figures without questioning them; 
   attackers exploit this conditioning deliberately.

3. FEAR
   "Your account has been compromised"
   "Legal action will be taken"
   "You owe back taxes"
   
   Why it works: Fear narrows thinking and creates 
   motivation to resolve the threat quickly without 
   fully evaluating the source.

4. SCARCITY
   "Only 24 hours remaining"
   "Limited slots available"
   "Offer expires today"
   
   Why it works: Scarcity creates perceived loss 
   which motivates faster decision-making than gain.

5. SOCIAL PROOF
   "Your colleague already completed this"
   "All staff are required to verify"
   
   Why it works: We look to others to validate 
   our own decisions; attackers create false consensus.

6. FAMILIARITY
   Emails that appear to be from known vendors, 
   colleagues, or services we use regularly.
   
   Why it works: We lower our guard with familiar 
   names and brands; attackers deliberately mimic 
   trusted contacts.
```

### 2.2 Phishing Detection Framework
```
THE S.T.O.P. FRAMEWORK FOR PHISHING DETECTION

S: SENDER
   Does the email address match the claimed sender exactly?
   Is the domain correct? (company.com vs company-secure.com)
   Is there a slight misspelling? (cornpany vs company)
   Did you expect an email from this person or organization?

T: TONE AND CONTENT
   Is the urgency level unusual for this type of communication?
   Does the language match how this person normally writes?
   Are there grammar or spelling errors that seem out of character?
   Is the request unusual for this person or organization?

O: OBJECTIVE
   What is this email actually asking you to do?
   Click a link? Provide credentials? Send money? 
   Share information?
   Would the legitimate organization actually 
   ask for this via email?

P: PAUSE BEFORE ACTING
   Can this request be verified through a separate channel?
   Is there any reason NOT to call and verify directly?
   What is the worst case if this is a phishing attempt 
   and I engage with it?

IF IN DOUBT: DO NOT CLICK. REPORT TO IT SECURITY.
```

### 2.3 Real Phishing Examples by Role

**For Operations and Logistics Staff**
```
SCENARIO: Carrier Invoice Phishing

You receive an email from what appears to be 
a carrier you work with regularly. The email 
says their banking details have changed and 
asks you to update payment information before 
processing the attached invoice.

RED FLAGS:
  Unsolicited change to payment details
  Attachment from unexpected email
  Domain is carrier-logistics.com 
  instead of actual carrier domain
  No phone call to confirm preceded the email

CORRECT RESPONSE:
  Do not open the attachment
  Do not update payment details
  Call the carrier's known number to verify
  Report the email to IT Security
```

**For Project Management Staff**
```
SCENARIO: Shared Document Phishing

You receive a notification that a stakeholder 
has shared a project document with you via 
Google Drive. Clicking the link takes you 
to a page that looks like Google login 
asking you to sign in to view the file.

RED FLAGS:
  Google login page URL is not accounts.google.com
  You were not expecting a document from this person
  The email notification domain is slightly off

CORRECT RESPONSE:
  Do not enter credentials
  Go directly to drive.google.com in a new browser tab
  Check if the document appears there
  Report the suspicious email to IT Security
```

**For Leadership**
```
SCENARIO: CEO Fraud / Business Email Compromise

You receive an email appearing to be from 
the CFO asking you to urgently approve a 
wire transfer to a new vendor before end 
of business today. The email says it is 
confidential and not to discuss with others.

RED FLAGS:
  Urgency combined with request for secrecy
  Wire transfer request via email without 
  prior discussion
  Request to bypass normal approval process
  "Do not discuss with others" instruction

CORRECT RESPONSE:
  Do not approve or initiate any transfer
  Call the CFO directly on their known number
  Report to IT Security regardless of outcome
  The instruction not to discuss is a manipulation tactic; 
  ignore it entirely
```

### 2.4 Vishing and Smishing

**Vishing (Voice Phishing)**

Vishing attacks have become significantly more 
sophisticated with the emergence of AI voice 
cloning technology. In 2023 a finance employee 
at a multinational company was tricked into 
transferring $25 million after a video call 
with what appeared to be senior colleagues; 
all were AI-generated deepfakes.
```
VISHING DEFENSE RULES

Legitimate IT staff will NEVER call you unsolicited 
and ask for your password or remote access.

Legitimate banks will NEVER ask you to 
confirm your full account number, PIN, 
or password over the phone.

Legitimate government agencies initiate 
contact via mail; not phone calls demanding 
immediate payment.

If a caller claims to be someone you know 
and makes an unusual request:
  Hang up
  Call them back on their known number
  Verify the request before acting
```

**Smishing (SMS Phishing)**
```
COMMON SMISHING SCENARIOS

Package delivery notifications with links
  (you may not be expecting a package)
Bank fraud alerts requiring immediate action
  (call your bank's known number instead)
Prize notifications requiring link clicks
  (you did not enter a contest)
Two-factor authentication codes requested by text
  (legitimate services never ask you to 
  share the code you receive)

RULE: Never click links in unsolicited text messages. 
Navigate directly to the organization's website 
using a browser instead.
```

### 3.2 Password Security Framework
```
THE ORGANIZATIONAL PASSWORD STANDARD

MINIMUM REQUIREMENTS
Length:       14 characters minimum
Complexity:   Uppercase; lowercase; numbers; symbols
Uniqueness:   Different password for every account
Change:       Immediately upon suspected compromise
Storage:      Organizational password manager only

PROHIBITED PRACTICES
Using personal information (name; birthday; pet)
Reusing passwords across accounts
Sharing passwords with anyone including managers
Writing passwords on paper near your workstation
Saving passwords in browser on shared devices
Using dictionary words even with number substitutions
  (p@ssw0rd is not a strong password)

STRONG PASSWORD EXAMPLES
Passphrase:   correct-battery-horse-staple-47!
Random:       T7#mK9@pL2$nQ4xR
Generated:    Use organizational password manager 
              to generate; do not create manually
```

### 3.3 Multi-Factor Authentication Deep Dive

MFA is the single most impactful account security 
control available to individual users. Microsoft's 
research shows MFA blocks 99.9% of automated 
account compromise attacks. Every account that 
supports MFA should have it enabled; no exceptions.
```
MFA METHODS RANKED BY SECURITY

MOST SECURE
Hardware security key (YubiKey or equivalent)
  Physical device required; nearly impossible to phish

HIGHLY SECURE
Authentication app (Microsoft Authenticator; Google Authenticator)
  Time-based codes that expire every 30 seconds
  Resistant to most phishing attacks

SECURE
Push notification to registered device
  One-tap approval from trusted device
  Vulnerable to MFA fatigue attacks if attacker 
  sends repeated push requests

LEAST SECURE (but better than nothing)
SMS text message code
  Vulnerable to SIM swapping attacks
  Still blocks the majority of automated attacks

ORGANIZATIONAL REQUIREMENT
Authentication app or hardware key required 
for all privileged accounts and any account 
with access to confidential or restricted data.
```

### 3.4 MFA Fatigue Attack Awareness
```
WHAT IS AN MFA FATIGUE ATTACK?

An attacker who has obtained your password 
repeatedly triggers MFA push notifications 
hoping you will eventually approve one out 
of frustration or confusion.

HOW TO RECOGNIZE IT:
You receive multiple unexpected MFA approval 
requests you did not initiate.

CORRECT RESPONSE:
Do NOT approve any request you did not initiate
Contact IT Security immediately
Change your password immediately
Report through SOP-003 Incident Response procedure

DO NOT approve a push notification just to 
make it stop. That is exactly what the attacker 
is counting on.
```

```
FOUR-TIER CLASSIFICATION SYSTEM

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

🟢 PUBLIC
Definition: Information approved for public distribution
Examples:   Marketing materials; published reports; 
            job postings; public website content
Storage:    No restrictions
Sharing:    No restrictions
Disposal:   Standard disposal

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

🔵 INTERNAL
Definition: For organizational use; not for external sharing
Examples:   Internal policies; meeting notes; 
            operational procedures; team communications
Storage:    Organizational systems only
Sharing:    Within organization only; no external sharing 
            without explicit approval
Disposal:   Secure deletion

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

🟡 CONFIDENTIAL
Definition: Sensitive information requiring access controls
Examples:   Financial data; personnel records; 
            vendor contracts; security assessments; 
            strategic plans
Storage:    Encrypted storage; access controlled
Sharing:    Need-to-know basis only; encrypted transmission
Disposal:   Secure deletion with confirmation; 
            physical documents shredded
Labeling:   Mark CONFIDENTIAL on all documents

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

🔴 RESTRICTED
Definition: Highest sensitivity; strictest controls required
Examples:   PII; payment card data; health information; 
            trade secrets; security vulnerabilities; 
            incident details
Storage:    Encrypted; access logged and audited
Sharing:    Explicit authorization required for every share; 
            encrypted transmission mandatory
Disposal:   Certified secure deletion; 
            physical documents cross-cut shredded
Labeling:   Mark RESTRICTED on all documents
Special:    Never transmitted via standard email

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

### 4.3 AI Tools and Data Classification

This is one of the most important data handling 
topics in the current environment and one that 
most organizations have not fully addressed yet.
```
AI TOOLS DATA HANDLING RULES

PUBLIC data:       May be used with any AI tool
INTERNAL data:     May be used with approved 
                   organizational AI tools only; 
                   not public consumer tools
CONFIDENTIAL data: Enterprise AI tools only; 
                   check with IT Security before use
RESTRICTED data:   Never input into any AI tool 
                   without explicit IT Security approval

WHAT THIS MEANS IN PRACTICE

Acceptable:  Pasting a generic process description 
             into ChatGPT to help draft an SOP
             (no real names; no specific data)

Not Acceptable: Pasting an actual employee record; 
                a real vendor contract with financial terms; 
                or actual customer data into any 
                public AI tool

When in doubt: Use placeholder data to get the 
               AI output; then replace placeholders 
               with real data in the final document 
               on a secured organizational system
```

### 5.2 Home Network Security Standard
```
HOME NETWORK SECURITY CHECKLIST

Router Security
☐ Default router password changed to strong unique password
☐ Router firmware updated to current version
☐ WiFi encryption set to WPA3 (preferred) or WPA2 minimum
☐ Guest network enabled and separate from primary network
☐ Remote management disabled unless specifically needed

Device Security
☐ Work device operating system fully updated
☐ Endpoint protection (antivirus and EDR) active and updated
☐ Screen lock configured; activates after 5 minutes idle
☐ Full disk encryption enabled
☐ VPN installed and tested

Physical Security
☐ Work device not left unattended outside home
☐ Screen not visible to others in shared home spaces
☐ Privacy screen used if regularly working in shared spaces
☐ Work documents not left visible or accessible to others

## MODULE 6: Incident Recognition and Reporting

### 6.1 Incident Reporting Statistics
```
THE COST OF DELAYED REPORTING

Average time to identify a breach:    204 days
Average time to contain a breach:      73 days
Total average time:                   277 days

Cost of breach identified in under 200 days: $3.93M average
Cost of breach taking more than 200 days:    $4.95M average
Difference:                                  $1.02M

Source: IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report 2023

Every day between a compromise and its discovery 
is a day the attacker has access to more data; 
more systems; and more opportunity to cause harm.

Early reporting is not just a policy requirement. 
It is one of the most financially significant 
security behaviors any employee can demonstrate.
```

### 6.2 What to Report and When
```
REPORT IMMEDIATELY (within minutes, not hours)

Security Events
  Suspicious email that you clicked or nearly clicked
  Unexpected account lockout or failed login attempts
  MFA push notifications you did not initiate
  Unusual computer behavior (slow; programs opening alone; 
  cursor moving without input)
  Suspected malware or ransomware

Physical Security Events
  Lost or stolen work device (laptop; phone; tablet; USB)
  Unauthorized person in a restricted area
  Tailgating through a secured entrance

Data Events
  Accidental email to wrong recipient containing 
  confidential or restricted data
  Unauthorized access to data you should not have seen
  Suspected data exfiltration by a colleague

Social Engineering Events
  Suspicious phone call requesting credentials or access
  Unusual request from someone claiming to be IT
  Request to share credentials or bypass security controls
```

### 6.3 How to Report
```
INCIDENT REPORTING PROCEDURE

Step 1: STOP what you are doing if an active 
        incident is in progress
        Do not click further; do not try to fix it; 
        do not delete evidence

Step 2: DISCONNECT from the network if you 
        believe malware is active on your device
        Turn off WiFi; unplug ethernet
        Do not power off the device

Step 3: CONTACT IT Security immediately
        Phone: [IT Security phone number]
        Email: [IT Security email]
        Slack: [IT Security Slack channel]
        Use whichever channel is fastest to reach them

Step 4: DOCUMENT what happened
        Time of incident
        What you observed
        What actions you took before reporting
        Any suspicious emails; screenshots if possible
        Names of anyone else involved or who witnessed it

Step 5: COOPERATE fully with the response team
        Provide your device if requested
        Answer questions honestly and completely
        Do not discuss the incident externally 
        until cleared by IT Security or Legal
```

IT and security staff have privileged access 
that represents the highest-value target in

TOP SECURITY RISKS FOR YOUR ROLE

Privileged account compromise with broad impact
Social engineering targeting IT staff specifically
Insider threat given broad system access
Shadow IT and unauthorized tool usage by staff
Supply chain attacks through vendor software

YOUR SPECIFIC SECURITY RESPONSIBILITIES

Use privileged accounts only for tasks requiring 
privileged access; use standard accounts for 
everyday activities including email and browsing.

Never perform privileged operations from a 
personal device or unsecured network regardless 
of urgency.

Treat all security incident details as RESTRICTED; 
share only on need-to-know basis and through 
secured channels.

Be especially skeptical of any request that 
seems to require you to bypass a security control; 
even from senior leadership. Verify through 
separate channels.

Document every privileged action taken on 
production systems; this is both a security 
control and a legal protection for you personally.
```

### TRACK D: Leadership and Management Security

**Key Risks for This Role**

Leadership accounts are specifically targeted 
in whaling attacks (executive phishing) and 
business email compromise because they have 
financial authority and organizational trust.
```
TOP SECURITY RISKS FOR YOUR ROLE

Whaling attacks impersonating executives
BEC attacks targeting financial authority
Deepfake voice and video attacks (emerging and real)
Pressure-based social engineering exploiting authority
Information disclosure through unsecured communications

YOUR SPECIFIC SECURITY RESPONSIBILITIES

Never approve a financial transaction requested 
via email alone; all wire transfers and payment 
changes require verbal confirmation through 
a known phone number.

Be aware that your name and authority will be 
used by attackers to pressure your staff. 
Establish with your team that you will never 
ask them to bypass security controls or act 
in urgent secrecy via email.

Treat your calendar and travel schedule as 
CONFIDENTIAL; attackers use executive schedules 
to time BEC attacks when targets are traveling 
and less available to verify requests.

Champion security culture publicly and visibly. 
Research consistently shows that security 
behavior improves when leadership models it; 
your visible engagement with these practices 
has an outsized impact on team behavior.
```
