## 💡 A Note Before You Start

Most security awareness training is boring, 
and that is the real security problem.

People sit through a compliance video once a year, 
click through the quiz at the end, and forget 
everything within a week. Meanwhile the phishing 
emails keep arriving, the passwords stay weak, 
and the organization keeps wondering why human 
error is still the leading cause of security incidents.

This guide is written differently. It is written 
for real people doing real jobs who need to 
understand security without becoming security experts. 
Every topic is explained in plain language with 
real examples that reflect situations you might 
actually encounter. There are no acronyms without 
explanations and no technical jargon without context.

The goal is not compliance. The goal is genuine 
awareness that changes behavior because you 
actually understand why it matters; not because 
someone told you it was required.

## MODULE 1: Why Security Is Everyone's Responsibility

### The Honest Truth About Security Incidents

The most sophisticated cybersecurity technology 
in the world can be bypassed by one employee 
clicking one wrong link. That is not an exaggeration; 
it is what the data shows year after year.

According to the Verizon Data Breach Investigations 
Report, human error is a factor in the majority 
of security incidents. Not because people are 
careless or unintelligent, but because attackers 
are skilled at creating situations that look 
completely normal right up until the moment 
they are not.

Security is not just the IT department's job. 
It never was. IT Security builds the walls and 
monitors the systems; but you are the one deciding 
whether to open that email, share that password, 
or plug in that USB drive you found in the parking lot.

### What Attackers Actually Want

Understanding what attackers are after helps 
make sense of why the things we ask you to 
do actually matter.

**Money** is the most common motivation. Ransomware 
attacks encrypt your organization's files and 
demand payment to restore them. Business email 
compromise tricks employees into wiring money 
to fraudulent accounts. Both start with a human 
making a mistake that seemed reasonable at the time.

**Data** is the second most common target. Customer 
records, employee information, financial data, 
and intellectual property all have value on 
criminal marketplaces. A single stolen database 
can fund an attack operation for months.

**Access** is what enables everything else. 
Attackers often spend weeks or months inside 
a network before anyone notices, quietly 
gathering credentials and mapping systems 
before they make their actual move.

### Your Role

You are not expected to be a cybersecurity expert. 
You are expected to be aware, skeptical, and 
willing to ask questions when something does 
not feel right. The single most valuable security 
behavior any employee can have is a healthy 
sense of "that seems a little off."

## MODULE 2: Phishing and Social Engineering

### What Phishing Actually Looks Like

Phishing is the practice of sending deceptive 
communications designed to trick you into 
revealing information, clicking a malicious 
link, or taking an action that benefits the attacker.

The word "phishing" makes it sound obvious and 
detectable. Modern phishing is neither. The emails 
look professional, the websites look legitimate, 
and the scenarios are carefully designed to 
create urgency that overrides careful thinking.

### The Most Common Phishing Scenarios

**The Urgent IT Request**
An email from what appears to be IT Security 
telling you your account will be locked unless 
you verify your credentials immediately by 
clicking the link provided. The urgency is 
deliberate; it is designed to make you act 
before you think.

**The Executive Request**
An email appearing to be from a senior leader 
asking you to complete a wire transfer, purchase 
gift cards, or share sensitive information. 
The sender's name looks right; the email address 
is slightly wrong if you look closely.

**The Vendor Invoice**
An email that looks like it is from a vendor 
you work with regularly, with an attached 
invoice or a link to update payment information. 
The attachment contains malware or the link 
leads to a credential-harvesting site.

**The Shared Document**
A notification that someone has shared a Google 
Doc, OneDrive file, or Dropbox folder with you. 
The link leads to a fake login page designed 
to capture your credentials when you sign in.

### How to Spot It
```
BEFORE YOU CLICK ANYTHING, CHECK:

Sender Email Address
   Does the domain match the organization exactly?
   look@company.com is legitimate
   look@company-security.com is suspicious
   look@cornpany.com (note the rn vs m) is a spoof

The Link Before You Click
   Hover over any link without clicking
   The actual URL appears at the bottom of your browser
   Does it match where it claims to go?

The Urgency Level
   Legitimate organizations rarely demand 
   immediate action under threat of consequences
   Urgency is a manipulation tactic; slow down

Something That Feels Off
   You do not recognize the sender
   The request is unusual for this person or organization
   The grammar or tone does not match how this 
   person normally communicates
   You were not expecting this communication
```

### What to Do When You Are Not Sure

Do not click. Do not reply. Do not call the 
number in the email.

Contact the sender through a separate, known 
channel; look up their phone number independently 
and call them directly to verify. Forward the 
suspicious email to IT Security before taking 
any action. If you are not sure, assume it is 
malicious until someone who knows confirms otherwise.

Reporting a phishing email that turns out to 
be legitimate costs nothing. Not reporting one 
that is malicious can cost the organization 
enormously. When in doubt, report it.

### Vishing and Smishing

Phishing does not only happen over email.

**Vishing** is voice phishing; a phone call from 
someone claiming to be IT Support, your bank, 
a government agency, or a vendor asking for 
information or access. Legitimate IT staff will 
never call you unsolicited and ask for your password.

**Smishing** is SMS phishing; a text message 
with a link or a request for information. 
Apply the same skepticism to unexpected text 
messages that you apply to unexpected emails.

## MODULE 3: Password and Account Security

### Why Passwords Matter More Than You Think

Your password is often the only thing standing 
between an attacker and everything your account 
has access to. A weak password is not just a 
personal risk; it is an organizational risk 
because your account has access to systems, 
data, and communications that affect the 
entire team.

### What Makes a Password Strong
```
STRONG PASSWORD CHARACTERISTICS

Length              At least 12 characters; longer is better
Complexity          Mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols
Uniqueness          Different password for every account
Unpredictability    Not based on personal information like 
                    birthdays, names, or common words

EXAMPLES

Weak:    password123
Weak:    JewelSmith1985
Weak:    Company2024!
Strong:  T7#mK9@pL2$nQ4
Strong:  correct-horse-battery-staple-47 (passphrase)
```

### The Password Manager Solution

Remembering a unique strong password for every 
account is not realistic without help. Password 
managers solve this problem by generating and 
storing strong unique passwords for every account 
so you only have to remember one master password.

Approved password managers for organizational 
use are listed in your IT Security policy. 
Using a personal password manager for work 
accounts should be cleared with IT Security first.

### Multi-Factor Authentication

Multi-factor authentication (MFA) means that 
logging in requires something you know (your password) 
combined with something you have (a code from 
your phone or an authentication app) or something 
you are (a fingerprint or face scan).

MFA is required for all organizational accounts 
and is one of the single most effective controls 
against unauthorized access. Even if an attacker 
obtains your password, MFA prevents them from 
logging in without also having physical access 
to your second factor.

Enable MFA on every account that supports it; 
work accounts, personal email, banking, and 
social media. The 10 seconds it adds to your 
login is worth it every single time.

### Account Security Rules

Never share your password with anyone including 
IT staff; legitimate IT support will never ask 
for your password. Never use work passwords 
for personal accounts or personal passwords 
for work accounts. Change your password immediately 
if you suspect it has been compromised and notify 
IT Security at the same time. Never save passwords 
in your browser on a shared or public computer.

## MODULE 4: Data Handling and Classification

### Not All Data Is Equal

Some information the organization holds is 
publicly available and carries minimal risk 
if shared. Other information is highly sensitive 
and could cause serious harm to the organization, 
employees, or customers if it fell into the 
wrong hands. Understanding the difference is 
fundamental to handling data responsibly.
```
DATA CLASSIFICATION LEVELS

PUBLIC
Information approved for public distribution.
Examples: Marketing materials, published reports, 
job postings.
Handling: No restrictions on sharing.

INTERNAL
Information for organizational use only; not 
for external distribution but not highly sensitive.
Examples: Internal policies, meeting notes, 
general operational information.
Handling: Do not share outside the organization 
without approval.

CONFIDENTIAL
Sensitive information that could cause harm 
if disclosed without authorization.
Examples: Financial data, personnel records, 
vendor contracts, security assessments.
Handling: Share only with authorized personnel 
on a need-to-know basis. Encrypt when transmitting.

RESTRICTED
Highly sensitive information requiring the 
strictest controls.
Examples: PII, payment card data, health information, 
trade secrets, security vulnerabilities.
Handling: Strict need-to-know access only. 
Encrypted storage and transmission required. 
Access logged and audited.
```

### Practical Data Handling Rules

Do not send confidential or restricted data 
via personal email or messaging apps. Do not 
store organizational data on personal devices 
or personal cloud storage without explicit IT approval. 
Do not discuss confidential information in 
public places where others could overhear. 
Lock your screen when stepping away from your 
workstation even for a few minutes. Shred physical 
documents containing sensitive information rather 
than placing them in regular recycling or trash.

### AI Tools and Data Handling

This is increasingly important as AI tools 
become part of everyday work.

Never paste confidential data, customer PII, 
employee records, financial figures, or sensitive 
security information into public AI tools like 
ChatGPT or Perplexity. These tools may use 
your inputs to improve their models and the 
information you submit cannot be recalled once submitted.

Use AI tools for drafting, research, and analysis 
with placeholder data. Bring real sensitive 
data in only after the output has been generated 
and only when necessary for final review.

## MODULE 5: Working Remotely and Securely

### The Remote Work Risk Profile

Working remotely introduces security risks that 
do not exist in a controlled office environment. 
Home networks are typically less secure than 
corporate networks, public wifi is actively 
hostile territory, and physical security 
is harder to control when your screen can 
be seen by family members, roommates, or 
strangers in a coffee shop.

### Home Network Security

Your home router is the entry point to your 
home network and by extension to any work 
you do from home. Change your router's default 
password if you have not already; default 
passwords are publicly known and are among 
the first things an attacker tries. Enable 
WPA3 encryption on your wifi network if 
your router supports it; at minimum use WPA2. 
Keep your router's firmware updated; manufacturers 
release security patches that protect against 
known vulnerabilities.

### Public Wifi Rules
```
PUBLIC WIFI RISK ASSESSMENT

Coffee Shops, Airports, Hotels: HIGH RISK
These networks are unencrypted and monitored 
by anyone with basic tools. Assume anything 
you transmit on public wifi can be seen.

SAFE ON PUBLIC WIFI
  General web browsing of public information
  Streaming media
  Reading email without sending sensitive content

NEVER ON PUBLIC WIFI WITHOUT VPN
  Logging into work systems or email
  Accessing financial accounts
  Submitting forms with sensitive information
  Any work-related activity involving organizational data

USE VPN
The organization's VPN encrypts your connection 
even on public wifi. Use it whenever you are 
working outside of your home or office network.
```

### Physical Security While Remote

Position your screen so it cannot be seen 
by others when working in public or shared spaces. 
Use a privacy screen filter if you regularly 
work in environments where screen visibility 
is unavoidable. Lock your computer when stepping 
away even at home; other people in your household 
should not have unintended access to work systems. 
Never leave work devices unattended in public 
spaces; a laptop left on a coffee shop table 
while you order is a theft opportunity.

## MODULE 6: Incident Reporting

### The Most Important Rule

If you see something, say something. Immediately.

The biggest cost in most security incidents is 
not the initial compromise; it is the time between 
the compromise and when someone reported it. 
Every hour an attacker spends undetected in 
a system is an hour of additional damage, 
additional data accessed, and additional 
cleanup required.

You will not be in trouble for reporting something 
that turns out to be nothing. You may contribute 
to a serious problem by not reporting something 
that turns out to be significant.

### What to Report
```
ALWAYS REPORT THESE IMMEDIATELY

Suspicious emails; especially those requesting 
credentials, payments, or urgent action

Unexpected account lockouts or login failures 
you did not cause

Unusual behavior on your computer; unexpected 
slowness, programs opening on their own, 
cursor moving without your input

A link or attachment you clicked that you 
now suspect may have been malicious

A request for information that seemed unusual 
coming from someone you know

A lost or stolen work device; laptop, phone, 
tablet, or USB drive

Anyone attempting to access areas or systems 
they should not have access to
```

### How to Report

Contact IT Security directly using the contact 
information provided during your onboarding. 
Do not attempt to investigate or remediate 
the issue yourself; doing so can contaminate 
evidence and make the response more difficult. 
Document what you observed including time, 
what you did, and what you noticed before 
reporting; this information helps the response team.

If you clicked a suspicious link or attachment, 
disconnect your device from the network immediately 
by turning off wifi and unplugging any ethernet 
cable, then contact IT Security. Do not turn 
off the computer; preserving the current state 
helps with forensic investigation.

### No Blame Culture

Organizations with strong security cultures 
treat incident reports as valuable intelligence, 
not as evidence of wrongdoing. The goal is 
to identify and stop threats as early as possible; 
that only happens when people feel safe reporting 
without fear of consequences.

Report early, report honestly, and let IT 
Security take it from there.

## MODULE 7: Knowledge Check

Answer the following questions to confirm 
completion of this training. Answers should 
be submitted through the training completion 
form linked in Notion.

**Question 1**
You receive an urgent email from what appears 
to be your CEO asking you to purchase $500 in 
gift cards immediately for a client surprise. 
What do you do?

a) Purchase the gift cards and email the codes as requested  
b) Reply to the email asking for more details  
c) Contact your CEO through a separate known channel 
   to verify before taking any action  
d) Forward the email to a colleague to ask their opinion  

**Question 2**
Which of the following is the strongest password?

a) Password2024!  
b) JewelSmith  
c) p@ssw0rd  
d) T7#mK9@pL2$nQ4  

**Question 3**
You are working at a coffee shop and need to 
log into a work system. What should you do first?

a) Connect to the coffee shop wifi and log in normally  
b) Use your phone as a hotspot or connect to the VPN before logging in  
c) Wait until you get back to the office  
d) Use a private browsing window on the coffee shop wifi  

**Question 4**
A colleague mentions they forgot their password 
and asks to use your login temporarily. What do you do?

a) Share your password since it is just this once  
b) Decline and direct them to IT Support to reset their own password  
c) Share your password but change it after they are done  
d) Create a temporary password they can use  

**Question 5**
You click a link in an email and the page 
asks for your work login credentials. The 
URL looks slightly different from usual. 
What do you do?

a) Enter your credentials since you need access  
b) Close the browser and forget about it  
c) Do not enter credentials; disconnect from the network 
   and report to IT Security immediately  
d) Enter your credentials but change your password afterward  

**Question 6**
Which of the following is safe to paste 
into a public AI tool like ChatGPT?

a) A customer's full name and address  
b) The organization's financial projections  
c) A generic description of a process with no real names or data  
d) An employee's personnel record  

---

**Answer Key**
1-c; 2-d; 3-b; 4-b; 5-c; 6-c

Score of 6 out of 6 required for training completion. 
Any score below 6 requires review of the relevant 
modules and a retake of the knowledge check.
